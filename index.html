About Me

I’m a student robotics engineer focused on competition-grade mechanism design, CAD, and iterative prototyping. Most of my work lives inside FTC robots: turreted shooters, linear extension systems, transfer mechanisms, and linkage-based structures that have to survive real matches.

I care about reliability, clean mechanical design, and systems that are predictable under stress. A lot of my process is: prototype fast, collect data, fix the weak points, and repeat until the mechanism behaves like a tool, not a gamble. This portfolio is a record of that progression across multiple FTC seasons.

DECODE™ (2025–2026 Season)

DECODE is an archaeology-themed FTC game centered around uncovering artifacts, matching Motif patterns, and managing scoring cycles. Robots collect 5-inch spherical artifacts and shoot them into alliance-specific corner goals.

Game Pieces

  • 36 total artifacts.
  • 24 purple, 12 green.
  • 5-inch spheres with holes for indexing and manipulation.

Primary Scoring

  • Robots intake artifacts and shoot them into corner goals.
  • High-value cycles reward accuracy and fast turnaround.

The Obelisk & Motifs

  • A central Obelisk displays a three-artifact Motif.
  • Alliances earn bonus points if their Classifier Ramp matches the Motif.
  • Requires controlled feeding and selective scoring.

Gate Management

  • Once the Classifier Ramp is full, teams actuate a Gate.
  • The Gate releases artifacts back onto the field.
  • Critical for maintaining high-value scoring cycles.

Endgame

  • Robots must return to their Base (18×8 inch square).
  • Points for partial and full parks.
  • Bonus if both alliance robots fit in the Base.

Key Constraints

  • Strict horizontal expansion limit.
  • Hardware cap: 8 motors and 10 servos.

INTO THE DEEP™ (2024–2025 Season)

INTO THE DEEP is an ocean-exploration-themed FTC game focused on collecting samples, scoring them in baskets, and performing endgame climbs on the Submersible structure.

Game Pieces

  • 80 plastic “samples” (triangular prisms).
  • Samples come in red, blue, and yellow.

Primary Scoring

  • Baskets: Robots score samples into low or high baskets in the corners.
  • Specimens: Human players convert samples into Specimens using clips, and robots hang them on high or low Chamber rungs.

The Submersible

  • Central structure where most samples are located.
  • Also used for endgame climbing tasks.
  • Requires precise navigation and alignment.

Endgame — Ascent

  • Robots climb the Submersible by hanging from one of three levels of rungs.
  • Higher levels give more points.
  • Robots must be fully supported by lower rungs before reaching Level 3.

Key Technology

  • Heavy use of AprilTags for navigation and alignment.
  • Vision-based positioning for consistent scoring and climbing.

Turreted Shooter System (V1–V3)

This shooter system went through three major iterations, each addressing mechanical inconsistencies, friction losses, compression tuning, and turret integration. The goal was to achieve consistent, repeatable shots from multiple field positions in DECODE.

Shooter V1 — Double Flywheel

V1 was a vertical double-flywheel shooter using dual high-RPM motors. It achieved strong exit velocity but exposed several issues that shaped later designs.

  • Inconsistent compression → inconsistent trajectories.
  • No angle adjustment → limited scoring positions.
  • Higher friction from wheel alignment and structure.
  • Heavy frame that was not turret-friendly.
Shooter V2 — Adjustable Hood + Counter Roller

V2 introduced an adjustable hood and a counter-roller system to improve control over shot angle and compression.

  • Adjustable hood enabled dynamic shot angles.
  • Counter-roller reduced backspin and smoothed feeding.
  • Improved compression tuning over V1.

Issues discovered:

  • Hood pivot friction slowed rebound and adjustment.
  • Compression drift at high RPM.
  • Belt tension inconsistencies under load.
Shooter V3 — Turret-Ready Final System

V3 focused on solving the remaining friction and consistency issues while integrating cleanly with the turret.

  • Thrust bearings added for smooth hood rotation.
  • Optimized compression curve for consistent trajectories.
  • Reduced inertia losses for faster recovery between shots.
  • Turret mounting pattern integrated into the base structure.
  • Friction minimized through refined roller geometry and alignment.

Park Mechanism (2025–2026)

The park mechanism was designed to secure reliable endgame points in DECODE by consistently returning the robot to the Base zone without interfering with the shooter or drivetrain.

At a high level, the mechanism prioritized low profile, fast actuation, and repeatability under match pressure. Details can be expanded as the design is finalized and documented.

  • Compact geometry to avoid conflicts with the turret and intake.
  • Fast deployment to maximize scoring time before endgame.
  • Designed to tolerate minor misalignment while still achieving a valid park.

Linear Extension System (2024–2025)

The linear extension system was a multi-stage mechanism designed for controlled vertical reach in INTO THE DEEP. It was used to interact with field elements at varying heights while staying within extension limits.

  • Multi-stage sliding structure with low-friction bearing surfaces.
  • Belt or string-driven actuation for compact routing.
  • Designed for consistent extension and retraction under load.
  • Integrated hard stops and guides to prevent binding.

Transfer System (2024–2025)

The transfer system handled the controlled handoff of game pieces from the intake to the scoring mechanism. It was tuned to reduce jams and maintain orientation of samples.

  • Smooth geometry to guide samples along a predictable path.
  • Compression tuned to avoid stalls while preventing misfeeds.
  • Designed to synchronize with the extension and scoring mechanisms.
  • Helped the team understand timing and mechanical coordination between subsystems.

Virtual 4‑Bar Mechanism (2024–2025)

The virtual 4‑bar mechanism used a combination of pivots and controlled geometry to approximate a 4‑bar motion path without a full traditional linkage. It was used where controlled end-effector orientation was important but space and complexity were limited.

  • Maintained approximate orientation of the end effector through its travel.
  • Provided a smooth, predictable motion path.
  • Reduced part count compared to a full 4‑bar linkage.
  • Served as a learning platform for motion planning and linkage design.
