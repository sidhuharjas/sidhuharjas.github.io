Shooting Mechanism

The shooting mechanism was the core scoring system for DECODE. It is mounted on a turret, allowing the robot to rotate independently of the drivetrain and score from multiple field angles without needing perfect alignment.

Across three major versions (V1, V2, V3), I iterated on compression, hood geometry, roller alignment, friction reduction, and turret integration to achieve consistent, repeatable shots under load.

Version 1

V1 was the initial prototype used to validate basic shooting geometry. It focused on compression testing, hood angle experiments, and roller spacing.

Version 2

V2 refined the hood curvature, reduced friction, and improved roller synchronization. This version was significantly more consistent than V1.

Version 3

V3 was the final competition‑ready shooter. It featured optimized compression, a stiffened hood, and clean turret integration for consistent scoring under load.

Transfer System

The transfer system moved samples from the intake to the scoring position in INTO THE DEEP. It went through three major iterations as I worked toward a design that was light, consistent, and tolerant to real‑match variability. Version 3 is the claw.

Version 1 — Passive Rubber‑Band Compression

V1 used a passive finger geometry with rubber‑band‑based compression. The rubber bands allowed the system to flex around the sample and guide it upward. Compression was adjustable by changing band count and mounting points.

What Worked

  • Very low weight
  • Simple, low‑part‑count geometry
  • Adjustable compression using rubber bands

What Didn’t Work

  • Unpredictable sample rotation
  • Required near‑perfect intake angle
  • No positive control — sample could slip or bounce

Version 2 — Torsion Spring + Micro‑Servo Gate

V2 introduced a torsion spring for more consistent preload and a micro‑servo gate to stabilize the sample during transfer. Compression remained adjustable by changing torsion spring preload.

What Improved

  • More consistent alignment due to torsion spring preload
  • Micro‑servo gate reduced bounce‑outs
  • Better tolerance to imperfect intake angles

Remaining Problems

  • Too heavy at the end of the arm
  • Servo horn flex under load
  • Snag points — exposed edges could catch on the sample’s triangular corners, causing jams during fast cycles

Version 3 — Lightweight Axon Mini Claw

V3 replaced the passive system with a compact Axon Mini–powered claw. The claw geometry was matched to the FTC sample, giving a large contact area and a stable grip. This version provided positive control at all times and eliminated the inconsistencies of V1 and V2.

What Made It Better

  • Extremely lightweight
  • Positive control at all times
  • Smooth, predictable handoff
  • No snag points — fully enclosed geometry
  • Fast actuation with the Axon Mini

Tipper Park Mechanism

The Tipper Park mechanism was designed for the DECODE endgame to reliably earn the Base ranking point. The system folds the robot backward into a parked configuration using a compact, servo‑driven linkage.

The mechanism is powered by two Axon Max servos working in parallel. Using dual servos provides significantly higher effective torque, reduces the load on each output shaft, and ensures the robot can consistently tip even under battery sag or when the robot is slightly misaligned. This redundancy also improves reliability during long events where single‑servo systems tend to overheat or stall.

FEA Study

Before manufacturing, I ran a finite element analysis (FEA) on the kicker plate to validate that the geometry could withstand the peak loads generated during tipping. This allowed me to remove unnecessary material while keeping the part stiff enough to avoid flex‑induced misalignment. The FEA confirmed that the highest stresses occurred near the pivot interface, so I reinforced that region while trimming weight from low‑stress areas.

Final Mechanism

The final design uses a reinforced pivot, MF4ZZ bearings for smooth rotation, and a compact linkage path that keeps the mechanism lightweight and consistent. The dual‑Axon‑Max drive provides smooth, controlled motion and prevents the robot from slamming into the ground during the tip. The result is a reliable, competition‑ready park mechanism that performs consistently across an entire event.

2‑Bar / Virtual 4‑Bar Mechanism

This mechanism moved samples from the intake to scoring positions in INTO THE DEEP. It is a simple 2‑bar linkage powered by an Axon Mini through a 1:1 aluminum gear.

An additional degree of freedom, which I refer to as pitch, allows the end of the arm to pivot and optimize the drop‑off position. The goal was a fast, predictable motion path with low mechanical complexity.
