Full Robot Overview

For the INTO THE DEEP competition robot, I led design and manufacturing of the V2 platform — responsible for the extension system, lift architecture, transfer claw, and deposit arm. The goal: design these subsystems to work seamlessly with my teammates' drivetrain, intake, and control systems.

Team Subsystems

  • Custom Drivetrain: Built using four 6061 aluminum plates custom cut on an industrial laser cutter to optimize chassis footprint.
  • Vertical Lift: Two synchronized motors geared at 5.7:1 with 3D printed sprockets and chain, doubling the previous lift speed. Utilizes a 3-stage MiSUMI structure and a down-string spring to manage slack.
  • Virtual 4-Bar Intake: Top-down, motor-driven intake. A chain mounted to a fixed shaft creates a virtual 4-bar, orienting the intake without a separate motor.
  • Intake Rollers: 3D-printed TPU fingers with hooks offset at 90° to automatically orient samples vertically. A bottom roller lined with surgical tubing acts like a vacuum to sweep up pieces.

Vertical Lift

The vertical lift is responsible for the longest physical movement in our scoring cycle and sees the highest volume of repetitions during a match. To hit aggressive cycle-time targets, I overhauled our lift architecture and load management system.

Dual-Motor MiSUMI Setup

Originally, a single motor was bottlenecking cycle speed. Moving to a dual-motor setup — combined with a change in the motor ratio — completely doubled the speed of the lift.

Mechanics & Reliability

  • Actuation: Two synchronized motors geared at 5.7:1, using custom 3D-printed sprockets and chain for perfectly matched power delivery.
  • Structure: 3-stage MiSUMI slide structure for rigidity and smooth travel under high lateral loads.
  • Down-string Tensioning: Counter-spring on the down-string dynamically adapts to slack caused by gravity assisting the descent, preventing derailments.

Counterspringing

Doubling the speed of the lift introduced a new problem: high current draw and increased stall risk. I implemented a mechanical counterspringing system to minimize load on the motors.

Gravity Neutralization

Constant-force springs attached to the moving stages neutralize the gravity load of the lift.

  • Reduced Motor Strain: Motors only need to overcome inertia and friction, not dead weight.
  • Battery Efficiency: Lower current draw prevents voltage sag during rapid cycling.
  • Faster Acceleration: Without fighting gravity, motors reach peak RPM much faster on the upward stroke.
Counterspringing Physics ΣFy = (m · g) − F_springs ≈ 0 m · g = total weight of moving stages + end effector F_springs = combined pull of constant-force springs → By zeroing the gravity penalty, 100% of motor torque is dedicated to acceleration (F = m · a)

Shooting Mechanism

The shooter is the most critical system in DECODE — it's responsible for scoring, so it has to be consistent, fast, and reliable. A single jam or miss during a critical match point can cost you the game.

My approach: iterate relentlessly. I built three major versions (V1, V2, V3), each time testing flywheel inertia, hood angles, friction losses, counter-roller dynamics, and turret mechanics. Every design decision was validated by testing.

Version 1 Prototype

First prototype: a double vertical flywheel system using two independently driven 6000 RPM motors with 4-inch wheels. While one advantage was inherent backspin mitigation, the design proved impractical for competition.

  • Accuracy Issues: Variations in motor output between the two wheels caused inconsistent ball trajectories.
  • Low Rate of Fire: Struggled to maintain consistent shooting speeds due to uneven power delivery.
  • Design Constraints: Fixed angle wheels and lightweight wheels made the launcher oversized, too heavy for effective turret mounting, with very little position flexibility.

Version 2 Iteration

V2 moved to a servo-actuated hooded design powered by dual 6000 RPM motors. A rack-and-pinion mechanism allowed precise hood angle adjustment between 35° and 55°, enabling fine-tuned shot trajectories.

Key Upgrades

  • Weight Reduction: Pocketed plates reduced overall mass by 0.75 kg without compromising strength.
  • Turntable Bearing: Geared lazy-susan bearing enabled smooth left-right turret rotation and acted as a receiver for artifacts.
  • Friction Reduction: Thrust bearings at all wall interfaces, lubricated with NLGI Grade 2 grease. Belts tensioned slightly loose to reduce drag.
  • Counter-Roller Introduction: Belted 30A shore hardness counter-roller spins clockwise to remove unwanted backspin, increase compression, and produce faster shots.

Version 3 Competition

V3 includes a multitude of friction, angle, and weight optimizations to minimize cycle time and maximize reliability. PID control ensures consistency across varying battery levels.

Major Refinements

  • Advanced Counter-Roller: Using a gear reversal system and HTD 3 belts, two custom molded top rollers spin at 9000 RPM to mitigate backspin.
  • Custom Silicone Molding: After Andymark 40A tore and Colson 30A reduced contact area, I created 3D printed molds and poured custom 2-part 30A silicone — consistent, leak-free parts.
  • Optimized Friction: GT2 and HTD3 belts replace HTD5. Thrust bearings everywhere clamping forces exist. Large radius herringbone gear actuates the hood around the wheel center.

Flywheel Inertia Analysis

Iterated through modeling clay (V1), solid disk (V2), and finally a pocketed ring (V3) to maximize inertia while minimizing weight.

System Inertia I = ½ · m · r² ≈ 0.00172 kg·m² Stored Energy at ω = 500 rad/s E ≈ 215 J Ball Kinetic Energy m = 0.0748 kg | v_exit = 20 m/s E_ball = ½ · m · v² ≈ 15 J → Only ~7% of stored energy transferred per shot. Wheel speed drop is minimal — rapid, consistent launches.

Tipper Park Mechanism

In DECODE, the Base ranking point is free points if you tip the robot at the end of the match. Most teams tried full-lift mechanisms, but they ran out of time or hit the 18-inch height limit. The solution: a small, fast tipping mechanism that secures this point reliably while staying simple.

The design uses two Axon Max servos in parallel for redundancy, torque multiplication, and reliability under battery sag. FEA was used to optimize the kicker plate — strong enough to handle the load, light enough to matter.

Finite Element Analysis

I ran a static FEA study on the kicker plate to ensure sufficient strength under the robot's weight, while allowing mass minimization through a strategic taper in areas with lower stress loads.

Linear Extension

The linear extension system provides the horizontal reach necessary to score samples in INTO THE DEEP. Designed to be fast, stable under load, and smoothly integrated with the transfer and intake mechanisms.

Design & Actuation

  • Power Delivery: Single Axon Max servo with a custom herringbone gear reduction for adequate driving force without stalling.
  • Herringbone Gear Profile: Self-centering during operation, smooth power transmission without side-load slipping or axial thrust forces.
Gear Reduction & Torque Multiplication Driving Gear = 16T | Driven Gear = 120T Ratio: 120 / 16 = 7.5:1 Reduction → 7.5× torque multiplication. Massive overhead overcomes slide friction and ensures consistent deployment under competition loads.

Transfer System

The transfer system takes samples from the intake and moves them to the deposit arm for scoring. It runs every cycle, hundreds of times per match. If it jams, slips, or misaligns — you lose time and points.

Three major iterations. V1 and V2 were passive systems that eventually hit their limits. V3 — the active vectoring claw — solved the core problem through active motor control and precision geometry. 50+ prototype versions went into this final design.

Version 1 — Passive Rubber-Band Compression

Passive finger geometry with rubber-band-based compression. Bands allowed the system to flex around the sample and guide it upward. Compression adjustable by changing band count and mounting points.

What Worked

  • Very low weight
  • Simple, low-part-count geometry
  • Adjustable compression using rubber bands

What Didn't Work

  • Unpredictable sample rotation
  • Required near-perfect intake angle
  • No positive control — sample could slip or bounce

Version 2 — Torsion Spring + Micro-Servo Gate

Introduced a torsion spring for more consistent preload and a micro-servo gate to stabilize the sample during transfer. Compression adjustable by changing torsion spring preload.

What Improved

  • More consistent alignment due to torsion spring preload
  • Micro-servo gate reduced bounce-outs
  • Better tolerance to imperfect intake angles

Remaining Problems

  • Too heavy at the end of the arm
  • Servo horn flex under load
  • Snag points — exposed edges caught on triangular corners, causing jams

Version 3 — Active Vectoring Claw

The most common culprit of lost time and reliability. Replaced passive systems with a compact Axon Mini-powered claw. Before arriving at this design, we designed, manufactured, and tested 50+ versions of both passive and active systems. Rigorous testing determined that active servo motion produced the highest performance.

Claw Geometry & Mechanics

  • Funneling Vector Geometry: Conforms to the shape of the sample and intake, actively vectoring pieces into position.
  • Maximized Surface Contact: Internal shape matches the sample perfectly for a large, stable grip.
  • Self-Centering Herringbone Gears: Multiple teeth contact simultaneously, drastically reducing chances of slipping or stripping.
  • Lightweight & Enclosed: No snag points, fully enclosed geometry for smooth and predictable handoffs.

2-Bar Deposit Arm

A 2-bar mechanism that moves the sample from the intake to the scoring baskets and bars. Powered at the back through a 1:1 aluminum gear connected to an Axon Mini to maximize movement speed.

Additionally, a pitch degree of freedom pivots the end of the arm to optimize the drop-off position. Goal: fast, predictable motion path with low mechanical complexity.

Torque & Payload Calculations

Required torque calculated at 51 cm extension length to ensure the arm could reliably lift the extension slides and end effector:

V1 — Horizontal Spinner

The horizontal spinner was my first ever combat robotics project — built to experiment with high-speed weapon systems, understand brushless motor behavior, and get hands-on with the electrical and mechanical challenges of spinning weapons before committing to a full robot design.

System Specs

  • Weapon disc: 140 mm diameter steel disc
  • Battery: 4S LiPo (14.8V nominal, 16.8V fully charged)

Weapon Physics

Why the Horizontal Spinner Was Abandoned

The horizontal spinner concept has a fundamental physics problem: gyroscopic deflection. A high-speed disc spinning in the horizontal plane resists any change to its axis of rotation. When the robot turns or gets hit, the gyroscopic force fights the chassis — making the robot nearly unsteerable and causing the frame to flex and twist unpredictably under impact.

  • Gyroscopic precession: Any applied torque perpendicular to the spin axis produces a reaction 90° away. Turning while the weapon spins causes the robot to pitch or roll instead of yaw cleanly.
  • Deflection under impact: When the disc hits a target, the reaction force couples with the gyroscopic effect to push the robot in unpredictable directions, making follow-up strikes unreliable.
  • Low wedge compatibility: Horizontal spinners sit high and present a large lateral surface. A low wedge opponent can slide underneath and negate the weapon entirely.

These aren't solvable by better chassis stiffness. They're geometric and physical constraints. The conclusion: move to a vertical spinner, where the gyroscopic effect actually aids ground contact and the weapon geometry is far more favorable.

Failure Analysis & ESC Lessons

The horizontal spinner phase destroyed a significant number of ESCs before I understood what was actually happening. This failure analysis is one of the most valuable engineering lessons of the project.

How the ESCs Were Dying

Every time the weapon disc hit something and decelerated rapidly — or when the motor was braked — a massive voltage spike appeared on the power rail and killed the ESC's MOSFETs. I went through multiple ESCs before I stopped and actually analyzed the circuit.

The Root Cause: Back-EMF & Stall Torque

  • Brushless motors are generators: When a spinning brushless motor decelerates suddenly — from impact or braking — the rotating magnetic field generates a back-electromotive force (back-EMF) spike. This voltage can far exceed the battery voltage and exceeds the MOSFET breakdown voltage, destroying the ESC instantly.
  • Stall torque vs. running current: Brushless motors at stall draw enormous current — sometimes 10–20× their running current — because there's no back-EMF to limit it. ESCs rated for the running current fail under stall conditions. Undersized ESCs couldn't handle the instantaneous current demand during weapon spin-up from zero or after a hard impact.
  • Brushed motors have the same issue: Brushed motors' commutator brushes arc under high current, and without protection, the motor controller sees the same inductive voltage spike on deceleration.

The Fix: Schottky Diode Flyback Protection

The solution was adding a Schottky diode — specifically a flyback (freewheeling) diode — across the motor terminals in the correct orientation.

  • How it works: When the motor decelerates and generates a back-EMF spike that exceeds battery voltage, the Schottky diode becomes forward-biased and provides a low-resistance path for the inductive energy to dissipate — bypassing the ESC's MOSFETs entirely.
  • Why Schottky specifically: Schottky diodes have a very low forward voltage drop (~0.2–0.4V vs ~0.7V for standard silicon diodes) and extremely fast switching. In a high-frequency back-EMF spike scenario, a slow diode fails to clamp the spike in time. Schottky diodes respond fast enough to catch the transient.
  • Orientation matters: The diode is placed with the cathode toward the motor's positive terminal. During normal operation it's reverse-biased and does nothing. During a back-EMF spike, it conducts and clamps the voltage.
Why the Schottky Diode Works V_spike = L × (dI/dt) — inductive spike proportional to inductance and rate of current change Without diode: V_spike flows through ESC MOSFETs → breakdown → destruction With Schottky diode: V_spike exceeds V_forward (~0.3V) → diode conducts → energy loops through motor → MOSFET protected → No more ESC deaths from back-EMF events

After implementing the diode circuit, ESC failures from back-EMF stopped entirely. This was one of the most important electrical engineering lessons of the project — understanding not just that components were failing, but why at the circuit level.

Vertical Spinner V1

After abandoning the horizontal spinner, I moved to a vertical eggbeater design. The eggbeater profile — a disc with material removed asymmetrically — is a proven combat weapon geometry that maximizes energy storage for a given mass while reducing the risk of the weapon getting stuck after a bite.

Why Vertical Over Horizontal

  • Gyroscopic advantage: A vertically spinning weapon generates gyroscopic forces that press the robot into the ground during forward movement, improving traction rather than fighting it.
  • Wedge compatibility: Vertical spinners sit low and pair naturally with a front wedge geometry. The wedge guides opponents up into the weapon's strike zone.
  • Impact geometry: Vertical hits transfer energy upward — launching opponents into the air — rather than sideways, where arena walls absorb the energy.

V1 Specs

  • Weapon: 80 mm diameter eggbeater
  • Battery: 4S LiPo
80mm Eggbeater Tip Speed Assuming weapon motor KV ≈ 2000 KV on 4S (14.8V nominal) RPM = 2000 × 14.8 = 29,600 RPM ω = 29,600 × (2π / 60) = 3,100 rad/s v_tip = 3,100 × 0.040 m = 124 m/s ≈ 277 mph Eggbeater profile reduces moment of inertia vs solid disc — faster spin-up, same tip speed

Why V1 Was Retired After 8 Chassis Iterations

The weapon geometry was sound. The chassis wasn't. V1's chassis was too large — overbuilt in ways that ate into the weight budget without adding meaningful structural benefit. I redesigned the chassis 8 times trying to solve it within the existing architecture, but the fundamental footprint was wrong. The lesson: a good weapon mounted on a bad chassis is still a bad robot. I scrapped the chassis entirely and started V2.

Vertical Spinner V2

V2 addressed the chassis size problem with a significantly smaller, lighter frame — but introduced new problems that took 15 iterations to fully diagnose.

Changes from V1

  • Weapon: 85 mm diameter eggbeater (slight size increase for more energy storage)
  • Chassis: Redesigned from scratch — smaller footprint for weight reduction
  • Drive motors: JustCuz Robotics brushed motors

Why V2 Still Failed

Problem 1 — Brushed Drive Motors

I used brushed motors for the drivetrain because they're simpler to control and cheaper. In practice, they were the wrong choice for this application:

  • High stall current: Brushed motors at low speed or under load draw enormous current. In a combat robot, the drivetrain stalls constantly — tight turning, pushing, being pushed. The motors ran hot and the ESCs struggled.
  • Low efficiency: Brushed motors waste more energy as heat compared to brushless equivalents. In a weight-limited robot where battery size is constrained, efficiency directly translates to runtime and speed.
  • Brush wear: Under repeated high-current stall events, brushes degrade quickly. Not ideal for a robot that gets hit repeatedly.

Problem 2 — Wrong Weapon Motor

I used a FingerTech Robotics inrunner at 2980 KV. The motor was physically too small for the application:

  • Inrunner vs outrunner: Inrunners have the magnets inside the rotating shaft. They spin faster but produce less torque at a given size. For spinning up a weapon disc with meaningful rotational inertia, torque during spin-up matters — an underpowered motor means slow weapon ramp-up and poor recovery after an impact.
  • Too small physically: The motor's stator couldn't produce enough torque to spin the 85 mm eggbeater to target speed quickly enough. Recovery time after impact was too long.

Problem 3 — Structural Integrity

Shrinking the chassis for weight savings compromised the structural integrity. The frame flexed under weapon torque reaction, and impact loads transmitted directly into drivetrain components. 15 iterations tried to patch this — gussets, different material thicknesses, geometry changes — but the core architecture had too many competing constraints to solve cleanly within V2.

V2 Assessment Chassis: 15 iterations, still structurally compromised Drive: Brushed — inefficient, high stall current, heat issues Weapon motor: 2980 KV inrunner — too small, slow spin-up, poor impact recovery → Architecture not salvageable. Moved to V3 with correct motor selection and redesigned chassis.

Why the BA 2320 1860 KV

  • Outrunner geometry: The rotating outer can gives the motor a larger effective radius for torque production compared to an inrunner of the same size — far better for spinning up a weapon with real rotational inertia.
  • 1860 KV on 4S: This KV rating hits the target tip speed window without over-revving or requiring additional reduction. Direct drive to the weapon keeps the system simple and eliminates belt/gear losses.
  • Spin-up torque: The BA 2320 has enough stall torque to bring the eggbeater from zero to fight speed in under 2 seconds — crucial for recovery after an impact that stalls the weapon.

Drivetrain

The drivetrain uses JustCuz Robotics Blitz Lites — brushless drive motors purpose-built for combat robots. The switch from the brushed motors in V2 to these was significant:

  • Efficiency: Brushless drive motors run significantly cooler and more efficiently under the repeated stall conditions of a combat match.
  • Weight: Blitz Lites are purpose-optimized for the weight class — small, powerful, and light.

See the for the full gear ratio analysis.

Battery — 4S 1100 mAh

Battery selection in combat robots is a delicate balance between energy, weight, and discharge rate. The 4S 1100 mAh cell was chosen after analysis:

  • 4S voltage: 14.8V nominal gives the weapon motor its target RPM band. Going to 6S would overspin the motor beyond its rated limits and require a different KV selection.
  • 1100 mAh capacity: A combat match is typically 3 minutes. At peak combined weapon + drive current (~40–60A burst), a 1100 mAh pack at 100C discharge rate has sufficient instantaneous current delivery. Going larger adds weight that hurts maneuverability. Going smaller risks voltage sag under load, which drops weapon speed mid-fight.
  • C rating: High-C LiPo cells maintain voltage under burst current loads. Voltage sag directly reduces weapon motor RPM and stored energy — a sagging battery is a slower weapon.
Runtime Estimate Capacity: 1100 mAh = 1.1 Ah Average combined draw (weapon cruise + drive): ~25A Runtime = 1.1 Ah / 25A × 60 = 2.64 minutes → Tight for a 3-minute match. Pack selection and power management are still being tuned.

Drivetrain & Ratio

The drivetrain in a combat robot isn't just about going fast — it's about controlled aggression. You need enough speed to close distance and dodge, enough torque to push and resist being pushed, and enough efficiency to survive a full match without browning out.

Optimal Gear Ratio

The target drivetrain tip speed is ~250 mph weapon spin with the robot itself moving at a controlled, maneuverable pace — roughly 4–6 mph for a robot in this weight class. Too fast and the robot is uncontrollable; too slow and it can't reposition after an attack.

Drivetrain Ratio Analysis — Blitz Lite on 4S Blitz Lite KV ≈ 3000–4000 KV (brushless, purpose-built drive motor) Free-spin RPM on 4S = 3500 × 14.8 ≈ 51,800 RPM (unloaded) Target wheel RPM for ~5 mph with ~30mm wheel: v = 5 mph = 2.24 m/s Wheel RPM = (v / (π × d)) × 60 = (2.24 / (π × 0.030)) × 60 ≈ 1,420 RPM Required reduction = 51,800 / 1,420 ≈ 36.5:1 → High reduction needed — achieved through motor gearbox + wheel size selection Torque at Wheel Motor stall torque × 36.5 reduction = massive torque overhead → Robot can push and resist being pushed without drivetrain stall

Why 250 mph Weapon Tip Speed is the Target

250 mph (~112 m/s) is a widely-used benchmark in combat robotics for vertical spinners in the antweight/beetleweight class. Here's why it matters:

  • Energy scales with v²: Kinetic energy is ½mv². Doubling tip speed quadruples stored energy. At 250 mph, even a small eggbeater stores enough energy to flip or damage most opponents.
  • Diminishing returns above 300 mph: Above ~300 mph, the gains in stored energy are offset by increased stress on the weapon, shaft, and mounting hardware. The weapon becomes more likely to self-destruct on a hard hit.
  • 250 mph is the sweet spot: Maximum competitive energy storage without risking weapon structural failure under impact loads at this weight class.

Weight Distribution

Weight distribution is as critical as total weight. In a combat robot, where and how weight is placed determines handling, stability, and how the robot responds to weapon recoil:

  • Low center of gravity: Mass concentrated low to the ground reduces the tendency to flip when the weapon bites. A high CoG robot gets launched on impact.
  • Rearward battery placement: The battery (heaviest single component) is placed toward the rear to counterbalance the weapon at the front. This keeps the CoG roughly centered and prevents the weapon from nose-diving the robot into the ground during spin-up torque.
  • Weapon reaction forces: When the weapon hits something, Newton's third law sends a reaction force into the chassis. The chassis must be designed so this force doesn't tip the robot over or flip it — which means the CoG placement and chassis geometry work together.
  • Drive wheel position: Drive wheels placed at the optimal lateral position maximize the turning moment while keeping the robot's rotational inertia low for quick pivoting.

Weapon Physics & Math

The eggbeater weapon geometry was chosen deliberately. Here's the complete physics breakdown of why it works, how it's sized, and what the numbers actually mean in a fight.

Why an Eggbeater Profile

A solid disc maximizes moment of inertia for a given diameter — more stored energy. But it also risks getting stuck in an opponent after a deep bite (the disc can't "ride over" the hit). The eggbeater profile removes material asymmetrically, creating a geometry where:

  • The weapon can bite into an opponent and the asymmetric shape causes it to "walk out" of the hit rather than jam.
  • Material removal reduces weight while keeping much of the rotational inertia (since most of the mass is still at the outer radius, where it contributes most to I = mr²).
  • The uneven mass distribution creates a slight imbalance — which, when properly tuned, is acceptable — but requires careful balancing to prevent destructive vibration at high RPM.

Progression: 80mm → 85mm → 95mm

Moment of Inertia Scaling (eggbeater approximation, constant mass) I ∝ r² — doubling radius quadruples I 80mm (r=40mm): I ∝ 0.040² = 1.600 × 10⁻³ 85mm (r=42.5mm): I ∝ 0.0425² = 1.806 × 10⁻³ (+13%) 95mm (r=47.5mm): I ∝ 0.0475² = 2.256 × 10⁻³ (+41% vs 80mm) → 95mm stores 41% more rotational energy than the original 80mm at the same mass and RPM Trade-off Larger diameter = lower free-spin RPM (tip speed is what matters, not RPM) Larger diameter = more stress at the weapon hub under impact loads → 95mm is the optimized point for this motor/battery combination

Wedge Geometry

The wedge is not a passive feature — it's an active weapon. The front wedge geometry is carefully designed to:

  • Undercut opponents: A low front wedge angle slides under opposing robots and lifts them, removing their traction and presenting them to the weapon. The wedge angle is typically 5–15° — shallow enough to get under most chassis gaps, steep enough to actually lift and guide.
  • Guide into the weapon: The wedge isn't flat — it's angled to direct an opponent upward and toward the eggbeater's strike zone. The geometry is designed so that once the wedge makes contact, the natural motion of the robot driving forward feeds the opponent into the weapon.
  • Self-righting aid: If the robot gets flipped (invertible design), the wedge geometry also helps it drive while inverted — the weapon can reach opponents in either orientation.
  • Low scrape angle: The wedge's contact point with the arena floor is kept as low as possible — ideally within 1–2mm — to prevent opponents from getting their own wedge underneath.
Wedge Geometry Design Target wedge angle: 8–12° from horizontal Leading edge height off floor: ≤ 2mm Wedge material: hardened steel or titanium — must resist opponent weapon hits without deforming → The wedge and weapon work as a system: wedge captures, weapon delivers energy

Structure & Materials

Every structural decision in this robot is a weight vs. strength trade-off. SolidWorks static studies, pocketing strategy, and TPU integration are the three tools I'm using to solve it.

SolidWorks Static Studies

Before cutting any material, I run static FEA studies on every structural component in SolidWorks. This is the same workflow I use in FTC (the Tipper Park kicker plate, for example) — applied here with much higher stakes because a combat robot's chassis sees impact loads that are harder to estimate from intuition alone.

  • Weapon mount stress: The weapon shaft and motor mount see the highest loads — weapon impact forces feed directly into these structures. FEA shows where yielding would occur first and guides material thickness decisions.
  • Chassis torsion: When one side of the drive gets hit while the other doesn't, the chassis twists. Static studies verify that the chassis cross-section resists this without permanent deformation.
  • Wedge interface: The joint between the wedge and the chassis is a high-stress point. FEA guides whether a gusset is needed and where exactly to place it.

Pocketing Strategy

Pocketing — removing material from low-stress regions of a part — is the primary weight-reduction method for structural components. The principle: keep material where the FEA shows high stress, remove it where stress is low.

  • Chassis side plates: Material is removed from the center spans between mounting points, where bending moment is lowest. The perimeter and mounting regions are kept full-thickness.
  • Weapon disc: The eggbeater profile is itself a form of pocketing — removing material between the "teeth" to reduce weight while keeping mass concentrated at the outer radius where it contributes most to rotational energy.
  • Motor mounts: Lightening holes where FEA shows near-zero stress, full material at the bolt bosses and load-bearing edges.

Pocketing in a combat robot is not the same as in a hobby part. Over-pocket and the part shatters on first impact. The FEA studies define exactly how much material can be removed before the safety factor drops below the acceptable threshold for combat loads.

TPU Integration

TPU (thermoplastic polyurethane) is a flexible, impact-absorbing 3D-printable material. In a combat robot, it plays a specific structural role:

  • Wheel guards / armor panels: TPU panels on the sides and rear absorb glancing impacts without shattering. Unlike rigid armor that transmits shock directly into the chassis, TPU deforms elastically and returns to shape, decoupling the impact energy from the frame.
  • Shock mounts: TPU printed at the right infill percentage acts as a vibration isolator between the weapon motor and the chassis, protecting electronics from high-frequency vibration during weapon spin-up and operation.
  • Weapon bumper: A thin TPU lip at the front of the chassis, just below the weapon, prevents opponent wedges from slipping under the weapon disc and hitting the motor mount directly.
  • Why not everywhere: TPU is heavy relative to its rigidity. It's used only where impact absorption outweighs the weight cost — primarily anywhere a glancing hit is more likely than a direct hit.

Innovation Project — Superpowered

Our challenge: identify and solve an impactful problem in the energy sector. We brainstormed across four categories — energy generation, transmission, storage, and consumption — and narrowed from over 50 identified problems down to one.

The Problem

Dust and soiling on solar panels reduces efficiency by 7–50%, costing solar farms between $242,000 and $1.6 million in lost revenue annually. In 2021, more than 10.8 billion gallons of water were used to clean solar panels — water that could have supplied 2 million people. Manual cleaning also claimed 11 lives in 2020.

Expert Research

  • Mrs. Carrie Beddle: Overview of the energy sector and identification of solution constraints.
  • Mr. Mike Hughes (Reliability First): Detailed breakdown of the power distribution and control system.
  • Dr. Kishore & Dr. Raji (Denora): Fuel cell alternatives to lithium batteries and hazards of radioactive materials.
  • Mr. Cody Cooper (Yellowlite): Challenges of solar installation and farm maintenance.
  • Great Lakes Energy Institute (Mr. Steirer & Mr. Raymond): Validated the solution as highly beneficial for large solar farms, especially in dry regions.
  • Amazon Solar Farms: Expressed strong support and promised collaboration on further development.

The Solution — Three-Part System

  • Bi-directional Cleaning Robot: Mounts directly onto solar panels and automates cleaning at predefined intervals, eliminating manual labor risk.
  • Water Harvesting & Filtration: Captures rainwater and recycles dirty runoff through a multi-stage filtration system — modeled after municipal wastewater treatment — for reuse.
  • Regional Chemical Customization: Soiling varies by geography. The system formulates and swaps targeted cleaning fluids to match the soil profile of different global regions, validated through soil simulation experiments.
