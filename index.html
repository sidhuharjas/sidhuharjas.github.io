Mechanisms Overview

I compete in the FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC), a global robotics program where teams design, build, and program 18‑inch robots to play a brand‑new game every season. Each game introduces new scoring rules, new field elements, and new engineering challenges, so the robot has to be engineered from scratch every year.

This page gives a simple story of the mechanisms I built across the 2024–2025 and 2025–2026 seasons and why they existed in the context of each game. Detailed technical breakdowns live on each mechanism’s dedicated page.

2025–2026 Season — DECODE™

DECODE is a shooting‑based game where robots collect 5‑inch spherical artifacts and score them into corner goals. The game rewards accuracy, consistency, and fast cycling, along with reliable endgame positioning.

What Robots Had to Do

  • Collect artifacts from the field.
  • Shoot them accurately into corner goals.
  • Navigate field zones for additional points.
  • Complete a Base park at the end of the match.

What I Worked On

  • Shooting Mechanism (V1–V3): turreted shooting system for multi‑angle scoring.
  • Tipper Park Mechanism: compact tipping solution for consistent Base ranking point.

2024–2025 Season — INTO THE DEEP™

INTO THE DEEP is a precision placement game where robots pick up triangular samples and score them into baskets or onto bars at different heights. My work focused on the mechanisms that moved and scored samples after the intake.

What Robots Had to Do

  • Collect samples from the field.
  • Lift them to multiple scoring heights.
  • Place them accurately into baskets or onto bars.
  • Complete endgame tasks such as climbs or parks.

What I Worked On

  • Linear Extension System: horizontal extension on a linear slide for scoring reach.
  • Transfer System: multiple iterations leading to a lightweight Axon Mini–powered claw.
  • Claw Geometry: sample‑matching claw with herringbone gears.
  • 2‑Bar / Virtual 4‑Bar: linkage for controlled motion to scoring positions.

Shooting Mechanism

The shooting mechanism was the core scoring system for DECODE. It is mounted on a turret, allowing the robot to rotate independently of the drivetrain and score from multiple field angles without needing perfect alignment.

Across three major versions (V1, V2, V3), I iterated on compression, hood geometry, roller alignment, friction reduction, and turret integration to achieve consistent, repeatable shots under load.

Version 1

V1 was the initial prototype used to validate basic shooting geometry. It focused on compression testing, hood angle experiments, and roller spacing.

Version 2

V2 refined the hood curvature, reduced friction, and improved roller synchronization. This version was significantly more consistent than V1.

Version 3

V3 was the final competition‑ready shooter. It featured optimized compression, a stiffened hood, and clean turret integration for consistent scoring under load.

Linear Extension System

The linear extension system was built for INTO THE DEEP to reach scoring heights on the Submersible and Chamber. It uses a horizontal servo‑powered linkage that rides on a linear slide.

The extension is geared 1:2.6 using two servo‑boosted Axon Max servos, providing enough torque to extend reliably under load while keeping the structure relatively lightweight.

Transfer System

The transfer system moved samples from the intake to the scoring position. It went through three major iterations as I worked toward a design that was light, consistent, and tolerant to real‑match variability.

Version 1 — Passive Finger Concept

V1 used passive geometry to guide the sample upward. It was extremely lightweight but inconsistent.

What Worked

  • Very low weight
  • Simple geometry
  • No electronics required

What Didn’t Work

  • Unpredictable sample rotation
  • Required perfect intake angle
  • No positive control

Version 2 — Hybrid Passive/Active

V2 added a micro‑servo gate for active stabilization. It improved alignment but introduced weight and flex issues.

What Improved

  • More consistent alignment
  • Servo gate prevented bounce‑outs
  • Better tolerance to imperfect angles

Remaining Problems

  • Too heavy at the end of the arm
  • Servo horn flex
  • Snag points

Version 3 — Lightweight Axon Mini Claw

V3 was the final competition‑ready version. It used a compact Axon Mini–powered claw that provided positive control over the sample with minimal weight and fast actuation.

What Made It Better

  • Extremely lightweight
  • Positive control at all times
  • Smooth, predictable handoff
  • No snag points
  • Fast actuation with the Axon Mini

Claw

The claw was designed to match the internal geometry of the FTC sample, giving it a large surface contact area and a firm, consistent grip.

Each finger uses a herringbone gear, which is self‑centering and allows multiple teeth to contact at once. This reduces the chances of slipping or stripping and keeps the motion smooth during opening and closing.

2‑Bar / Virtual 4‑Bar Mechanism

This mechanism moved samples from the intake to scoring positions in INTO THE DEEP. It is a simple 2‑bar linkage powered by an Axon Mini through a 1:1 aluminum gear.

An additional degree of freedom, which I refer to as pitch, allows the end of the arm to pivot and optimize the drop‑off position. The goal was a fast, predictable motion path with low mechanical complexity.
